1. How to fast

The core protocol: very low calories (most of them protein), almost no carbohydrate, very little fat. Typical window is 1–4 weeks, depending on body composition and goals.

  • Protein — roughly 1.2–1.5 g per kg of current body weight, split across 3–4 meals.
  • Carbs — under 20 g per day, ideally from leafy greens only.
  • Fat — incidental only, from the protein sources themselves.
  • Water — at least 35 ml per kg of body weight per day.

Hunger fades after the first 3–5 days as ketones rise. Energy stabilises. Stop if you feel weak, dizzy, or unwell — refeed gradually.

2. Preparing for low-carb eating

Two weeks before the fast, reduce carbs gradually. A sudden cut from a high-carb baseline causes the worst of the "keto flu" — fatigue, headache, irritability. The fix is a glide path, not a cliff.

  • Week −2 — remove sugars and refined flours. Keep some whole-grain carbs.
  • Week −1 — cut starches. Keep only fibrous vegetables.
  • Day 0 — protein-only.

Stock the kitchen with what you will actually eat. Remove the snacks that will tempt you on day 4.

3. Electrolyte supplementation

The single most common cause of feeling awful on a low-carb fast is electrolyte loss, not lack of food. Insulin drops when carbs drop, and the kidneys flush sodium. You have to replace it deliberately.

  • Sodium — 3–5 g per day (about 1.5–2.5 teaspoons of salt, spread across the day).
  • Potassium — 1–3 g per day (leafy greens, avocado, or supplement).
  • Magnesium — 300–400 mg per day (glycinate or citrate, evening).

A simple daily drink: 1 teaspoon salt + 500 mg potassium chloride in 1 litre of water, sipped through the day. Magnesium taken as a tablet at dinner.

4. Tools to prepare

The fast goes smoother when the friction of measuring, weighing, and decision-making is removed up front. The checklist above lists every item you will need.

Two notes that the checklist cannot fit:

  • The AI assistant. Set it up so the launch is one tap from the lock screen. The reason matters — every time you stand in a shop wondering whether something fits the plan, friction wins and you eat the wrong thing. Removing that friction is a multiplier on every other choice you make.
  • The sweetener. Stevia drops are the safest because pure stevia has no caloric load and almost no GI footprint. Stevia + erythritol blends work for many people but produce bloating or worse on an empty stomach for some. Test before day 1, not on day 4.
5. How to weigh yourself accurately

A daily weight is one data point against a noisy background. The point of the calibration tasks in the checklist above is to know the noise so you can read the signal.

Protocol.

  • Same scale, same spot on the floor, every time.
  • Morning, after using the toilet, before eating or drinking. This is your cleanest daily number.
  • If you must weigh in clothes, log the clothes weight too — the form in the tracker has a field for it.
  • Add a note to the entry whenever the context is unusual: "after dinner", "post-workout", "weighing in coat".

Knowing the fluctuation.

  • Toilet delta — typically a few hundred grams. Measured once and remembered.
  • Hourly drift without water — roughly 100 g/hour from sweat and breath. Measured by weighing yourself at 7 am and again at 10 am with nothing in between.
  • Clothing range — your lighter and heavier outfits, weighed once, become a quick lookup.

Once you know these three numbers, the daily reading stops being mysterious. A morning weight that is 400 g up is not a "gain" — it could be the toilet not done yet or the heavier outfit. The signal lives in the weekly trend, not the daily noise.

Important. A protein-sparing modified fast is a real medical intervention. Talk to a doctor before starting, especially if you take medication, are pregnant, are under 18, or have any kidney, liver, heart, or eating-disorder history. This app is a tracker and a working guide, not medical advice.
